How to Compare ESL Manufacturers: 2026 Buyer’s Guide

May 8, 2026·10 min read·AiESL Research

Choosing an ESL supplier is a multi-year commitment to a hardware fleet and a software platform. The cheapest label is rarely the cheapest system. Use these ten criteria to compare manufacturers on what matters over the life of the deployment.

The two kinds of ESL vendor

Most suppliers fall into one of two camps. Closed single-vendor systems lock the labels, the software and the protocol together — simple to buy, expensive to escape. Open-platform vendors run on standard protocols so one console can manage labels from many manufacturers. The distinction shapes every other decision below.

Ten criteria to compare

  • Openness & lock-in — does the platform run only its own hardware, or can it manage mixed-vendor fleets? Open systems protect the labels you already own.
  • Battery life — ask for a verified figure at a stated update frequency, not a best-case number. 5–10 years at 2–4 updates/day is a strong benchmark.
  • Display range — a full size ladder from ~1.5″ hooks to 10″+ promo formats, and color depth (black/white/red/yellow up to 7-color).
  • Update speed — how fast a store-wide price change reaches every label. Sub-second is the bar.
  • AI & dynamic pricing — is there a real decision engine with rules and guardrails, or just a scheduler?
  • Integration — native ERP/POS/WMS connectors and an open SDK/API, not a closed import tool.
  • Environmental rating — IP54 for general retail, IP67 and sub-zero ratings for cold chain.
  • Certification — CE, FCC, RoHS and ISO quality systems for global deployment.
  • MOQ & lead time — minimum order quantities and the ability to phase a rollout store by store.
  • Track record — deployed store count, supported countries and reference customers in your format.
The lock-in test
Ask any vendor one question: “Can your platform drive a competitor’s labels?” If the answer is no, you are buying a walled garden — and a future migration bill.

Total cost over five years, not unit price

A label that is a few cents cheaper but forces a full rip-and-replace at upgrade time is not cheaper. Weigh hardware against migration cost, integration effort and the labor the platform saves. Reusing existing labels through open middleware can cut migration cost by more than half.
